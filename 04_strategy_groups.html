Legal.

Your yarn
Red
Using courts, litigation, and constitutional argument to dismantle segregation through judicial decisions. The slow, expensive, generational work of building case law strong enough to topple Plessy.
Your task
For each event below, decide which image(s) on the wall represent the mechanism that event was targeting. Run red yarn from your event card to the image(s). On the index card attached to the yarn, write: the event, the year, the mechanism it targeted, and 1–2 sentences explaining the connection.
Your events
1909
NAACP founded
A coalition of Black activists (W.E.B. Du Bois, Ida B. Wells) and white reformers founds the NAACP in response to the 1908 Springfield, IL race riot. Its Legal Defense Fund will spend the next four decades building the case law that brings down Plessy.
naacpldf.org/about-us/history
1948
Shelley v. Kraemer
Supreme Court rules that racially restrictive covenants in property deeds cannot be enforced by courts. The deeds remain on the books; what changes is that no court will enforce them. NAACP victory argued by Thurgood Marshall.
oyez.org/cases/1940-1955/334us1
1954
Brown v. Board of Education
Supreme Court overturns Plessy v. Ferguson, ruling segregated schools "inherently unequal." Five consolidated cases, including Briggs v. Elliott from Clarendon County, SC. Decision is unanimous.
oyez.org/cases/1940-1955/347us483
1957
Little Rock Nine
Nine Black students integrate Central High School in Little Rock, AR, three years after Brown. Governor Faubus calls out the Arkansas National Guard to block them; Eisenhower federalizes the Guard and sends the 101st Airborne to escort the students to class.
nps.gov/chsc
1964
Civil Rights Act
Bans discrimination in public accommodations, employment, and federally funded programs. Signed by LBJ on July 2, 1964. The legal architecture of Jim Crow — the signs, the separate facilities, the legal segregation — becomes federally illegal.
archives.gov/milestone-documents/civil-rights-act

Economic.

Your yarn
Blue
Using collective economic pressure — boycotts, withholding labor, redirecting consumer power — to make segregation financially unsustainable. The strategy that runs on the dollar.
Your task
For each event below, decide which image(s) on the wall represent the mechanism that event was targeting. Run blue yarn from your event card to the image(s). On the index card attached to the yarn, write: the event, the year, the mechanism it targeted, and 1–2 sentences explaining the connection.
Your events
1940s
"Don't Buy Where You Can't Work" campaigns
Black communities in cities across the North and South boycott white-owned businesses that refused to hire Black workers. The campaigns built the organizing networks that Montgomery and others would later draw on.
blackpast.org · Don't Buy Where You Can't Work
1955–56
Montgomery Bus Boycott
After Rosa Parks's arrest on December 1, 1955, the Women's Political Council (Jo Ann Robinson) and the Montgomery Improvement Association organize a 381-day boycott of city buses. Black ridership was 75% of the system's revenue.
kinginstitute.stanford.edu/montgomery-bus-boycott
1956
Tallahassee Bus Boycott
Sparked by the arrest of Wilhelmina Jakes and Carrie Patterson, two FAMU students who refused to give up their seats on a Tallahassee bus. The boycott lasted seven months.
floridamemory.com (search "Tallahassee bus boycott")
1962
Albany Movement boycotts
In Albany, GA, Black residents boycott segregated buses, white-owned downtown businesses, and segregated facilities. The city closed parks rather than integrate them — but it taught the SCLC lessons that shaped Birmingham.
snccdigital.org · Albany Movement
1968
Memphis Sanitation Workers Strike
After two Black sanitation workers were crushed to death in a malfunctioning truck, 1,300 Memphis sanitation workers go on strike. "I AM A MAN" placards. Dr. King traveled to support the strike and was assassinated there on April 4, 1968.
aflcio.org · Memphis Sanitation Strike

Direct Action.

Your yarn
Yellow
Public, often nonviolent confrontation with segregation through bodies in space. Sit-ins, marches, freedom rides — putting the system on television and forcing the country to watch.
Your task
For each event below, decide which image(s) on the wall represent the mechanism that event was targeting. Run yellow yarn from your event card to the image(s). On the index card attached to the yarn, write: the event, the year, the mechanism it targeted, and 1–2 sentences explaining the connection.
Your events
1960
Greensboro sit-ins
Four students from North Carolina A&T sit at the Woolworth's lunch counter in downtown Greensboro and refuse to leave. By the end of the week, hundreds of students join. Within two months, sit-ins spread to 55 cities in 13 states.
sitinmovement.org
1961
Freedom Rides
Black and white CORE activists ride interstate buses through the South to test the Supreme Court's ruling against segregation in interstate travel. Riders are beaten in Anniston, AL (their bus is firebombed), Birmingham, and Montgomery.
nps.gov/articles/the-freedom-riders.htm
1963
Birmingham Campaign
SCLC and Birmingham activists target what King called "the most segregated city in America." Police Commissioner Bull Connor turns fire hoses and police dogs on schoolchildren. The images go national. Within months, Birmingham desegregates downtown facilities.
kinginstitute.stanford.edu/birmingham-campaign
1963
March on Washington
250,000 marchers gather at the Lincoln Memorial. King delivers the "I Have a Dream" speech. The march helps build pressure for the Civil Rights Act of 1964. Organized largely by Bayard Rustin, A. Philip Randolph, and the labor movement.
archives.gov · March on Washington
1965
Selma to Montgomery marches
Three attempted marches from Selma to the Alabama state capital. The first, on March 7, is broken up by state troopers on the Edmund Pettus Bridge ("Bloody Sunday"). The third, completed under federal protection, helps secure passage of the Voting Rights Act five months later.
nps.gov/semo

Electoral.

Your yarn
Green
Building Black political power through voter registration, party-building, and contested participation in democratic institutions — at enormous personal risk in places where registering to vote could get you killed.
Your task
For each event below, decide which image(s) on the wall represent the mechanism that event was targeting. Run green yarn from your event card to the image(s). On the index card attached to the yarn, write: the event, the year, the mechanism it targeted, and 1–2 sentences explaining the connection.
Your events
1962
Council of Federated Organizations (COFO)
SNCC, CORE, NAACP, and SCLC form a coalition to coordinate voter registration in Mississippi, where Black voter registration was below 7%. Registrars use literacy tests, poll taxes, and economic retaliation; activists are threatened, beaten, killed.
snccdigital.org · Mississippi
1964
Mississippi Freedom Summer
SNCC and COFO bring 1,000 mostly white college student volunteers to Mississippi to register Black voters and run Freedom Schools. Three workers — James Chaney, Andrew Goodman, Michael Schwerner — are murdered by Klansmen in Neshoba County.

1964
Fannie Lou Hamer at the DNC
The Mississippi Freedom Democratic Party challenges the all-white official MS delegation at the Democratic Convention in Atlantic City. Hamer's televised testimony — describing the beating she received for trying to register — is so powerful that LBJ calls a press conference to keep her off the air.
americanradioworks.publicradio.org · Hamer
1965
Lowndes County Freedom Organization
In a county that was 80% Black with zero registered Black voters, SNCC's Stokely Carmichael helps organize an independent Black political party. The party's symbol — a black panther — is later adopted by the Black Panther Party for Self-Defense.
snccdigital.org · LCFO
1965
Voting Rights Act
Federal legislation banning literacy tests and authorizing federal oversight of voter registration in covered jurisdictions. Black voter registration in Mississippi rises from 7% in 1964 to 60% by 1968.
archives.gov · Voting Rights Act

Institution
Building.

Your yarn
Purple
Creating Black-led organizations, schools, and infrastructure that develop leaders and sustain the movement across decades. The work that kept the rest of the movement running.
Your task
For each event below, decide which image(s) on the wall represent the mechanism that event was targeting. Run purple yarn from your event card to the image(s). On the index card attached to the yarn, write: the event, the year, the mechanism it targeted, and 1–2 sentences explaining the connection.
Your events
1932
Highlander Folk School founded
Myles Horton founds Highlander in rural Tennessee as an integrated training center for labor organizers and, later, civil rights activists. Rosa Parks attended a workshop there four months before her arrest in Montgomery.
highlandercenter.org
1957
Citizenship Schools (Septima Clark)
Clark develops a model for teaching Black adults in the South to read and to pass voter literacy tests. By 1969, an estimated 700,000 Black voters had been registered through Citizenship School graduates.
avery.cofc.edu · Septima Clark
1957
SCLC founded
After the Montgomery Bus Boycott, ministers from across the South form the Southern Christian Leadership Conference. King is its first president. SCLC becomes one of the movement's main organizing bodies.
kinginstitute.stanford.edu · SCLC
1960
SNCC founded (Ella Baker)
After the Greensboro sit-ins, Ella Baker convenes student activists at Shaw University in Raleigh. SNCC is founded as a Black-youth-led organization. Baker insists on bottom-up democratic organizing rather than charismatic leadership.
snccdigital.org · Ella Baker
1964
Freedom Schools
During Freedom Summer, COFO operates 41 Freedom Schools across Mississippi serving 2,500 students. The curriculum included Black history, current events, and political organizing. Several former students went on to organize in their own communities.
snccdigital.org · Freedom Schools

Armed
Self-Defense.

Your yarn
Orange
Organized armed protection of Black communities, organizers, and homes against white terror. Distinct from offensive violence — rooted in the right to self-defense and often working alongside, not against, nonviolent organizers.
Your task
For each event below, decide which image(s) on the wall represent the mechanism that event was targeting. Run orange yarn from your event card to the image(s). On the index card attached to the yarn, write: the event, the year, the mechanism it targeted, and 1–2 sentences explaining the connection.
Your events
1957
Robert F. Williams & the Monroe NAACP
Williams, president of the Monroe, NC NAACP chapter, organizes Black veterans into an armed defense unit after repeated Klan attacks. When the Klan motorcades through the Black neighborhood in 1957, the unit returns fire. Klan night-riding in Monroe ends.
historyisaweapon.com · Negroes With Guns
1962
Negroes with Guns published
Williams publishes a book arguing that armed self-defense is compatible with — and sometimes necessary for — nonviolent civil rights organizing. The book influences a generation of activists, including Huey Newton and Bobby Seale.
historyisaweapon.com · Negroes With Guns
1964
Deacons for Defense and Justice founded
In Jonesboro, LA, Black men — many of them WWII and Korea veterans — organize an armed group to protect CORE workers, civil rights marches, and Black neighborhoods from Klan violence. Chapters spread across Louisiana and Mississippi.
snccdigital.org · Deacons for Defense
1965
Bogalusa, Louisiana
The Bogalusa Deacons protect CORE workers during a sustained civil rights campaign in a town where the Klan operated openly. After several armed standoffs, federal marshals are deployed. Bogalusa becomes one of the first major Southern victories under the new Civil Rights Act.
snccdigital.org · Bogalusa
1965
Mississippi Freedom Summer protection
Throughout Freedom Summer 1964 and after, local Black communities — often grandmothers and farmers — provided armed nighttime guard at the homes where SNCC and CORE workers slept. The nonviolent movement's survival in the rural South depended on this protection.
Charles Cobb, This Nonviolent Stuff'll Get You Killed
