At the bus station, Durham, North Carolina
Photographer: Jack Delano

Made for the federal Farm Security Administration's photography project. Delano documented segregated transportation across the South. The signage in this image — "Colored Waiting Room" — was mandated by North Carolina state law.

South
Colored entrance, Crescent Theatre, Belzoni, Mississippi
Photographer: Marion Post Wolcott
Date: October 1939
Source: FSA/OWI · Library of Congress
Belzoni is in the Mississippi Delta, then a center of cotton sharecropping. The Crescent Theatre had two entrances: the main entrance and an outdoor staircase to the balcony marked "Colored Entrance." Black patrons paid the same admission, used a separate door, sat above.

South
Colored water cooler, streetcar terminal, Oklahoma City
Photographer: Russell Lee
Date: July 1939
Source: FSA/OWI · Library of Congress
One of the most reproduced photographs in the FSA archive. Note the difference in scale between the two coolers — separate but visibly unequal. Oklahoma's segregation statutes covered transportation, schools, hospitals, prisons, and public accommodations.

4
South
Sharecropper family, Deep South
Photographer: Dorothea Lange
Date: 1936–1937
Source: FSA/OWI · Library of Congress
Sharecropping replaced slavery as the dominant labor system in the cotton South after Reconstruction. Tenants worked land owned by white landlords, paid in a share of the crop, and were kept perpetually in debt to plantation stores. Black sharecroppers had little legal recourse against fraud.

5
South
Briggs v. Elliott plaintiffs, Summerton, South Carolina
Photographer: Cecil Williams
Date: June 17, 1951
Source: Civil Rights Digital Library
Plaintiffs gathered at Liberty Hill AME Church. Briggs was the first of the five cases consolidated into Brown v. Board of Education (1954). Clarendon County spent roughly $221 per white student and $45 per Black student; many Black children walked miles to one-room schools while white children rode buses.

6
North · Newark, NJ
HOLC residential security map, Essex County
Source: Home Owners' Loan Corporation
Date: 1939
Archive: Mapping Inequality, U. of Richmond
A federal agency graded neighborhoods A (green), B (blue), C (yellow), D (red) for mortgage risk. D-grade areas — disproportionately Black — were denied federal mortgage insurance for decades. Newark's redlines map almost exactly onto today's homeownership and school funding gaps.
→ Analyze this image
7
North · Cicero, IL
Cicero riot — the Clark family
Date: July 1951
Sources: Chicago Tribune archives;
BlackPast; Zinn Education Project
Harvey Clark, a Black bus driver, signed a lease for an apartment in all-white Cicero, a Chicago suburb. A white mob of 4,000 attacked the building over three nights. The National Guard was called. No rioters were charged; instead, a grand jury indicted the Clarks' lawyer and the apartment owner.
→ Analyze this image
8
North · Detroit, MI
The Birwood Wall (Eight Mile Wall)
Photographer: John Vachon
Date: August 1941
Source: FSA/OWI · Library of Congress
A six-foot concrete wall built in 1941 between an existing Black neighborhood and a planned white subdivision. A developer needed federal FHA loan approval; the FHA required physical separation from the Black neighborhood as a condition. The wall still stands today.
→ Analyze this image
9
North · Levittown, PA
The Myers family with neighbors, Levittown
Photographer: Charlotte Brooks
Date: 1957
Source: LOOK Magazine · Library of Congress
William and Daisy Myers were the first Black family to move into Levittown, PA — a planned suburb of 17,000 homes built with federal financing. Levittown's deeds contained restrictive covenants barring sale to non-whites. The Myers family was met by mobs, burning crosses, and stones for months.
→ Analyze this image
10
North · Washington, DC
American Gothic — Ella Watson
Photographer: Gordon Parks
Date: August 1942
Source: FSA/OWI · Library of Congress
Parks was the first Black photographer hired by the FSA. Ella Watson was a custodian at the federal building where Parks worked. He photographed her in front of a US flag, holding a broom and mop — a deliberate echo of Grant Wood's painting that named the daily-life dimension of segregation in the nation's capital.

How to use these cards
One context card accompanies each printed image on the wall. The card supplies verified facts only — photographer, date, location, brief context. Analysis happens on the Image Analysis card (Doc. IA–01) and on the index cards students post next to their image.

The cards are color-coded along the top: brick for Southern sources, blue for Northern and Western sources. The color matches the side of the wall where the image belongs.
